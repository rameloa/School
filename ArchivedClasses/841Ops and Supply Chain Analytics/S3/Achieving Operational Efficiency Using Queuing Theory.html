No one likes waiting to be served. Generally, we have varying tolerances for waiting depending on the context. If I'm at the hospital and need my appendix removed, I'm "happy" to hang around and wait hours until a surgical suite is available for me. Conversely, if I'm waiting to board a plane that is arriving late, I'm probably somewhat cranky right away. 

There are consequences to an organization that forces customers to wait. If I've had repeated experiences with a specific airline departing late, I might be compelled to purchase my next flight from an alternative airline. That implies that the airline has lost a potential sale - an opportunity loss to the airline.

How does the airline adapt to ensure more on-time departures? They would need to expand the fleet and change their network to increase the slack within the network, permitting longer turn-times at airports so that an early morning flight being late doesn't persist to be late throughout the remainder of the day's flights. Naturally, adding aircraft to a fleet is very expensive. 

Queue Modelling Using the Sakasegawa Approximation

As a manager (or analyst supporting the manager), what we'd like to do is identify the minimum cost solution - or the "sweet spot" balancing "service costs" (e.g., the salary of cashiers working at Costco) with "waiting costs" (e.g., the implied loss when a customer gets tired of waiting and chooses to shop somewhere else next time). Central to this is identifying how long a customer is expected to wait given some number of "servers" (e.g., cashiers). This can be determined using the Sakasegawa approximation.   2. Decrease the variability in the customer arrivals, thereby reducing the corresponding coefficient of variation,  C V I A T {"version":"1.1","math":"\( \tiny CV_{IAT}\)"}
  3. Decrease the variability in the service time, thereby reducing the coefficient of variation  C V S T {"version":"1.1","math":"\( \tiny CV_{ST}\)"}

In the second of the above options, this is purpose of scheduling arrivals, like when you make an appointment at a specific time to see your doctor. 

In the third of the above options, a manufacturer might switch from producing customized clothing to a standardized production process where a production process consistently makes the same sized shirt repeatedly until switching to the next size. 

While the first of the above options costs us money, options two and three are free at the operations level (although may have associated costs at the strategic level).
